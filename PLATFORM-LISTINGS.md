### 5 Sample Helpful Comment Templates

**Template 1: Spreadsheet replacement question**

> *Use when someone posts about outgrowing spreadsheets or managing operations in Google Sheets/Excel*

```
I've seen this exact problem a lot with small businesses. A few options depending on your budget:

1. **Airtable or Notion** — good middle ground if you want something more structured than a spreadsheet but aren't ready for a custom tool. Limited once your logic gets complex though.

2. **Custom-built web app** — if your process is genuinely unique (which it sounds like it is), getting something purpose-built can be surprisingly affordable. I work at a small studio that does exactly this kind of work (JMS Dev Lab — we're a Shopify Partner but build custom tools too). Happy to answer questions about what that process looks like if it'd help.

3. **No-code tools like Retool or Budibase** — can work for internal tools, but you'll hit walls on permissions and complex logic.

The key question is: does off-the-shelf software actually fit your workflow, or are you going to spend months bending it to kind-of-work?
```

**Template 2: Shopify cashflow / finance question**

> *Use when someone posts about managing finances, cashflow, or forecasting for their Shopify store*

```
Spreadsheets work until they don't — especially when you're trying to forecast and your data is always a week behind.

A few things that might help:

- **Shopify's built-in analytics** cover the basics (revenue, orders, etc.) but they're not really cashflow tools.
- **Bench or Pilot** are good for bookkeeping but expensive and not real-time.
- **SmartCash** (full disclosure: built by my team) syncs your Shopify orders automatically and gives you a live cash position, AI forecasting, and what-if scenarios. It's $9.99/mo so pretty low risk to try. smartcashapp.net if you want to look.

But honestly, the most important thing is having *any* system that updates automatically. Manual cashflow tracking in spreadsheets always falls behind, and by the time you update it, the data is stale.
```

**Template 3: Jewelry business workflow question**

> *Use when someone in r/jewelry or related subs asks about managing commissions, client work, or business operations*

```
Custom commissions are tricky to track because every piece has its own timeline and client preferences. I've seen studios try:

- **Trello/Asana** — okay for basic pipeline tracking, but they don't understand jewelry-specific details (ring sizes, metal choices, etc.)
- **Generic CRMs like HubSpot** — overkill and not designed for this at all
- **Spreadsheets** — they work until you have 50+ active commissions and then it's a nightmare

Full disclosure: my company (JMS Dev Lab) built a tool specifically for this — JewelryStudioManager. It's a Shopify app that tracks commissions, stores client preferences, and has a portal where clients can check their piece's progress instead of calling you. But I'd only recommend it if you're on Shopify.

If you're not on Shopify, something like Trello with a custom board structure is probably your best free option.
```

**Template 4: Staff training / onboarding question**

> *Use when someone asks about training new staff, onboarding processes, or team management for their store*

```
The biggest mistake I see is trying to do training via a shared Google Doc or a "shadow someone for a week" approach. Both fall apart when you hire your third or fourth person.

What works better:

- **Break it into modules** — product knowledge, POS system, customer service scripts, opening/closing procedures. Make each one completable in 15-30 minutes.
- **Add simple quizzes** — not to be corporate, but to catch gaps before they become customer-facing mistakes.
- **Document once, reuse forever** — the time investment upfront saves weeks over the next year.

For Shopify stores specifically, we built an app called StaffHub that does exactly this (training modules, quizzes, scheduling, and performance reviews in one place — $4.99/mo). But even without a tool, just having a structured, written training plan puts you ahead of 90% of small shops.
```

**Template 5: Marketing overwhelm / where to start question**

> *Use when someone asks about marketing strategy, feeling overwhelmed, or not knowing where to begin*

```
The problem isn't usually *what* to do — there's no shortage of marketing advice. The problem is doing it consistently when you're also running the actual business.

What's worked for the small business owners I've talked to:

1. **Pick one channel** and do it well for 90 days before adding another. Trying to be on Instagram, TikTok, email, SEO, and Pinterest simultaneously is a recipe for burnout.
2. **Time-box it** — 30 minutes a day maximum. If you can't do a marketing task in 30 minutes, break it down further.
3. **Use templates and AI** — ChatGPT can draft social posts and email copy. It's not perfect but it's better than a blank page.

We actually built an app (GrowthMap) around this exact idea — it gives you three small marketing tasks a day, completable in under 30 minutes, with AI-drafted content. Think of it as Duolingo for marketing. It's on Shopify if you want to try it: mygrowthmap.net.

But even without a tool, the "one channel, 30 minutes a day" approach works.
```

### What NOT to Do (Avoid Bans)

1. **Never create posts that are just product pitches.** Every post should be a genuine question, insight, or discussion contribution. Self-promotional posts get removed and can get you banned.

2. **Never comment on a thread just to drop a link.** Every comment must provide genuine value first. The product mention should feel like a natural aside, not the point of the comment.

3. **Always disclose your affiliation.** "Full disclosure: I built this" or "my team made this" is mandatory. Astroturfing (pretending to be a random user recommending your own product) will get you permanently banned and damage your reputation.

4. **Never use multiple accounts** to upvote your own comments or create fake discussions. Reddit detects this and bans all accounts.

5. **Never reply to every relevant thread in the same subreddit on the same day.** It looks spammy. 2-3 genuinely helpful comments per subreddit per week is a sustainable pace.

6. **Never argue with people who criticise your product.** Respond gracefully, acknowledge feedback, and move on. Arguments in public threads are a lose-lose.

7. **Never DM people unsolicited** to pitch your products. This violates Reddit's rules and annoys people.

8. **Don't just participate in product-relevant threads.** Build karma and credibility by answering questions on topics you know well even when there's no product to mention. You want to be known as a helpful person who also happens to build software — not as a marketer in disguise.

9. **Respect subreddit-specific promotion rules.** Some subs have dedicated self-promotion threads (e.g., r/SaaS monthly share threads). Use those channels when available rather than shoehorning promotions into regular threads.

10. **Don't use the same comment copy repeatedly.** Moderators notice patterns. Each comment should be genuinely tailored to the specific question being asked.

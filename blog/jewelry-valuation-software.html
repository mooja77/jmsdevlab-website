Jewelry Valuation Software: What to Look For

12 March 2026

If you offer jewelry valuations, the quality of your certificates directly affects your reputation. A handwritten certificate or a half-formatted Word document might contain accurate information, but it signals to the customer — and their insurer — that your operation is informal. Insurers reject poorly formatted certificates regularly. Customers lose confidence when they receive something that looks like it was thrown together in ten minutes. Dedicated valuation software solves both problems at once: it produces professional, insurance-compliant certificates and cuts the time you spend on each one dramatically.

Why Valuation Software Matters

There are four reasons to move beyond manual certificate creation.

Professional appearance. A well-designed certificate with your logo, consistent formatting, and clear layout tells the customer you take their valuation seriously. It also tells their insurance company that you are a credible valuer. First impressions matter, and for many customers, the certificate is the only tangible thing they walk away with.

Insurance compliance. Insurers have specific expectations about what a valuation certificate must contain. Missing a required field — or presenting information in an ambiguous way — can lead to the certificate being rejected. Software that enforces required fields eliminates this risk.

Time savings. Creating a certificate manually involves typing out descriptions, formatting the document, attaching photographs, and exporting to PDF. With the right software, most of that work is automated or templated. What used to take 30–45 minutes can take under 10.

Consistency. When you produce certificates manually, formatting drifts over time. Different staff members use slightly different wording. Font sizes change. Photographs end up in different positions. Software enforces a single standard across every certificate you issue.

Essential Features to Look For

Not all valuation software is equal. Here are the features that actually matter when you are choosing a tool.

Standardised certificate templates. The software should provide pre-built templates that follow industry conventions. You should not need to design your own layout from scratch. Look for templates that include your business branding — logo, contact details, and valuer credentials — automatically.

Required fields. A proper valuation certificate needs specific information. The software should prompt you for all of it and flag anything that is missing before you generate the final document. At a minimum, you need:

  • Detailed item description (metal type, gemstone details, hallmarks)
  • Materials and their quality (e.g., 18ct yellow gold, VS1 clarity diamond)
  • Measurements and weight
  • Photographs — multiple angles, ideally with a scale reference
  • Replacement value with the valuation date
  • Valuer details, qualifications, and signature

PDF export. Every certificate should be exportable as a clean, print-ready PDF. This is non-negotiable. Customers need to send certificates to their insurers, and PDF is the universal format for that.

Customer delivery portal. Emailing PDFs works, but a customer portal is better. It gives your customers a secure place to access their certificates at any time, reduces your support burden when someone loses their copy, and looks more professional than an email attachment.

Insurance Compliance: What Insurers Actually Require

Insurers reject valuation certificates more often than most jewellers realise. The most common reasons are straightforward: missing information and vague descriptions.

An insurer needs to be able to identify the exact item from the certificate alone. That means the description must be specific enough to distinguish the piece from similar items. "Gold ring with diamond" is not sufficient. "18ct white gold solitaire ring set with one round brilliant-cut diamond, approximately 0.75ct, colour G, clarity VS2, claw setting" is what they need.

The replacement value must be clearly stated as a retail replacement figure, not a resale or scrap value. The date of valuation must be present — most insurers require certificates to be less than three years old. The valuer's qualifications and professional memberships should be listed. Photographs should show the item clearly, and ideally include any identifying marks or hallmarks.

When certificates are created by hand, one or more of these details tends to get missed. It is not always obvious which fields an insurer considers mandatory until the certificate gets bounced. Good valuation software builds all of these requirements into the template so you cannot accidentally skip them.

Time Savings: Minutes Instead of Hours

Consider the manual workflow for a single valuation. You examine the piece, take notes, photograph it, then sit down at a computer to type up the certificate. You open your Word template, fill in the fields, resize and insert the photographs, adjust the formatting, export to PDF, and email it to the customer. For a single piece, this easily takes 30 to 45 minutes of desk work after the examination itself.

With dedicated software, the workflow is different. You enter the item details into structured fields — no formatting to worry about. You upload photographs and the software places them correctly. You hit a button and get a finished PDF. The same certificate takes five to ten minutes. If you do five valuations a day, that is two or more hours saved. Over a week, that is an entire working day recovered.

The time savings compound further when you factor in re-valuations. If a customer returns for an updated valuation, the software already has their previous certificate on file. You update the replacement value and any changed details, and the new certificate is ready almost immediately.

Integration With Your Store

If you run your jewelry business on Shopify, your valuation software should ideally connect to your existing setup. When your customer data, product catalogue, and order history already live in Shopify, re-entering that information into a separate system is wasted effort.

Look for software that can pull customer details directly from your Shopify store. This means no duplicate data entry, fewer errors, and a single source of truth for your customer records. If the software also syncs completed valuations back to the customer's profile in Shopify, even better — your front-of-house staff can see at a glance whether a customer has an existing valuation on file.

What About Generic Document Tools?

You can create valuation certificates in Word, Google Docs, or any other document editor. Plenty of jewellers do. But there are real drawbacks to this approach.

Generic tools have no validation. Nothing stops you from generating a certificate with a missing replacement value or no valuer signature. You will only discover the omission when the insurer rejects it and the customer calls you.

Formatting is fragile. Word templates break when photographs are inserted at different sizes. Pagination shifts. Fonts substitute on different machines. What looks fine on your screen may look wrong when the customer opens it.

There is no central record. Certificates live as individual files on your hard drive or in a shared folder. Finding a specific customer's certificate from two years ago means searching through folders. There is no portal, no search, and no audit trail.

Generic tools work in a pinch, but they create ongoing friction that dedicated software eliminates entirely.

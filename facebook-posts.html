Value Post 1 — Spreadsheet Struggles

"5 things jewelers track in spreadsheets that break when the business grows"

I've spent the last few years building software for independent jewelers, and there's a pattern I see over and over: a studio starts small, tracks everything in spreadsheets, and then hits a wall around 15-20 active orders.

Here are the five things I see break first:

1. Custom commission pipeline
When you've got 3 commissions, a spreadsheet is fine. When you've got 15 in various stages — some waiting on approval, some sourcing stones, some in production, some ready for delivery — things start falling through the cracks. Someone's anniversary ring gets forgotten because the row was scrolled off-screen. No formula can replace a proper pipeline view.

2. Client preferences & measurements
Ring sizes, metal preferences, allergies, past purchases, "she likes art deco but not yellow gold." Try keeping that in a spreadsheet across 200+ clients and finding it when they walk in. Most jewelers end up relying on memory, which works until it doesn't.

3. Repair ticket status
Paper tickets or spreadsheet rows for repairs create one guaranteed problem: "is it ready yet?" phone calls. There's no easy way to update the customer, no easy way to track where a piece is in the process, and no record of what was communicated when.

4. Valuation certificate records
Valuations need specific fields for insurance companies to accept them. Spreadsheets don't enforce that, so you end up with inconsistent certificates and customers calling back because their insurer rejected the paperwork. Keeping a searchable archive of past valuations? Forget it.

5. Staff training progress
If you've got bench staff or sales floor team, tracking who's been trained on what, who needs refreshers, and who's certified for specific tasks gets messy fast in a spreadsheet. It's usually the first thing that just stops getting updated.

We ended up building apps for #1-4 specifically for Shopify jewelers — happy to share what we learned if anyone's interested. But honestly, even if you don't use our stuff, the main thing is recognising when the spreadsheet has become the bottleneck instead of the tool.

What's the first thing that broke for you?

Value Post 2 — Insurance-Ready Valuations

"What makes a valuation certificate insurance-ready? Here's the checklist"

Had a jeweler tell me last month that a customer came back furious because their insurer rejected a valuation certificate. Turns out the certificate was missing two fields the insurer required. It happens more often than you'd think.

So here's the checklist of what most UK and US insurers want to see on a valuation certificate. If yours covers all of these, you're solid:

The essentials:

  • Detailed item description — not just "gold ring with diamond." They want the specifics: style, setting type, number of stones, band width, any hallmarks.
  • Metal type and purity — 9ct, 14ct, 18ct, 750, 950 platinum, etc. Include weight in grams.
  • Gemstone details — for every stone: type, cut, carat weight, clarity grade, colour grade. For diamonds, this means the 4Cs explicitly stated.
  • Measurements — dimensions of the piece and individual stones (mm).
  • Photographs — at least one clear photograph of the item. Some insurers want multiple angles.
  • Replacement value — current retail replacement cost, not what the customer paid. This needs to be a realistic market figure.
  • Valuer credentials — your qualifications, professional memberships (FGA, DGA, GIA, NAJ, etc.), and business details.
  • Date of valuation — insurers typically want revaluations every 2-3 years.
  • Unique certificate number — for reference and record-keeping.

Common rejection reasons I've seen:

  • Missing gemstone grades (especially clarity and colour on diamonds)
  • No photograph attached
  • Replacement value with no explanation of how it was determined
  • No valuer credentials or membership numbers
  • Certificate older than 3 years

We actually built this entire checklist into a Shopify app called Jewel Value so nothing gets missed — it won't let you issue a certificate until all required fields are completed. But even without software, printing this list and keeping it next to your bench is a good start.

Anyone else had certificates rejected? What was the reason?

Value Post 3 — Repair Ticket Case Study

"How one workshop replaced their paper repair tickets"

Wanted to share a story that might resonate with anyone still using paper repair tickets or a notebook system.

A watch repair workshop I worked with — small operation, owner and two bench staff — was processing about 40-50 repairs a month. Every repair got a handwritten ticket. Carbon copy for the customer, original pinned to the job bag. Pretty standard setup.

The problems weren't dramatic. They were slow and constant. A ticket would get separated from its job bag. Someone would take a phone call asking "is my watch ready?" and have to physically walk to the bench to check. The owner spent Friday afternoons going through tickets to figure out what was overdue. Quoting was done on the phone with no written record, which meant occasional disagreements about what was agreed.

The worst part? They lost a customer's vintage Omega for three days. It was on the bench the whole time — the ticket had just ended up in the wrong pile. The customer was understandably panicked. Nothing was damaged, but the trust took a hit.

They switched to a digital system with a simple pipeline: received, diagnosed, quoted, approved, in progress, ready for collection, collected. Each repair got logged with photos at intake. When a piece moved to a new stage, the customer got an automatic email or SMS notification.

Within a month, the "is it ready?" calls dropped dramatically. The Friday afternoon ticket audit disappeared — overdue jobs were flagged automatically. Quoting was done through the system with a written record both sides could reference. And nothing got lost, because every piece was logged with photos the moment it came in.

The owner told me the biggest surprise wasn't the time saved — it was that customers started leaving better reviews. Turns out, people really value being kept in the loop without having to chase.

Full disclosure: we built the app they used — it's called RepairDesk and it runs on Shopify. But the principle works regardless of what tool you use. If you're still on paper and doing more than 20 repairs a month, a digital pipeline will change your life.

Anyone else made this switch? How did it go?

Helpful Comment Templates

Use these as starting points when replying to other people's posts. Always personalise — reference something specific in their post. These should feel like a genuine reply, not a sales pitch.

Remember: The goal of commenting is to be helpful and build credibility. If you can help without mentioning your apps, do that. Only mention an app if it's directly relevant and you've already provided genuine value in the comment.

1. Someone struggling with commission organisation

When to use: Someone posts about losing track of custom orders, forgetting client details, or commissions falling through the cracks.

This is really common once you get past 10-15 active commissions. Spreadsheets just aren't built for pipeline tracking. What helped a few jewelers I've worked with: get everything into a system with stages (enquiry → design → approval → production → delivery) so you can see at a glance what needs attention. If you're on Shopify, feel free to DM me — I built something for exactly this and happy to show you.

2. Someone asking about digital vs paper repair tickets

When to use: Someone asks how others track repairs, or complains about paper ticket problems.

Made this switch with a workshop doing about 40 repairs a month. Biggest wins: automatic customer notifications (kills the "is it ready?" calls), photo documentation at intake (protects you if there's a dispute), and a simple pipeline view so you can see every active repair at a glance. The transition takes about a week to get used to, but nobody I've worked with has gone back to paper.

3. Someone frustrated with valuation paperwork

When to use: Someone mentions valuation certificates taking too long, insurers rejecting certs, or looking for a better process.

Insurance rejections usually come down to missing fields — gemstone grades, photographs, or valuer credentials being the most common. If you make a checklist of what insurers require and work through it for every cert, rejection rates drop to nearly zero. Happy to share the checklist we use if that would help — we ended up building it into an app but the checklist itself is useful on its own.

All designed around how jewelry studios actually work rather than trying to adapt a generic tool. 5. General "how do you manage [X]?" question

When to use: Someone asks a general question about managing some aspect of their jewelry business (inventory, clients, workflow, etc.).

Depends on volume, honestly. Under 10-15 active [jobs/clients/orders], most people get by with spreadsheets or even a notebook. Past that, you start losing things. The key thing is having one place where you can see everything at a glance and know what needs attention today. What's your current volume like? That would help narrow down what might work for you.

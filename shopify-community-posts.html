How-To Post 1 — "How We Built a CRM Specifically for Jewelry Studios"

Post in: Shopify Community > Shopify Apps > Building an app

Title: How We Built a CRM Specifically for Jewelry Studios

I wanted to share the story behind an app we built, because the problem it solves is one I think a lot of niche merchants deal with — and I'd love feedback from anyone in the jewelry space.

About a year ago, I started talking to custom jewelry studios on Shopify. The same problems kept coming up. They were tracking commissions in spreadsheets — one column for the client, one for the stone, one for the setting, one for "where are we in the process." Some had 200+ rows. When a customer emailed asking "how's my ring coming along?", the jeweler had to dig through the spreadsheet, figure out the status, and write a reply. Every single time.

Client preferences were scattered across email threads, text messages, and Post-it notes. A repeat customer would come back, and the jeweler would have to ask all the same questions again — ring size, metal preference, budget range — because none of it was in one place.

There was also no progress tracking that the customer could see. The whole process felt opaque to the client, which meant more emails, more calls, and more time spent on communication instead of crafting.

So we built JewelryStudioManager. It's a Shopify CRM designed specifically for studios that take custom commissions. It tracks each commission through a pipeline (enquiry, design, production, quality check, delivery), syncs with Shopify customers and orders, and gives clients a branded portal where they can check their piece's progress, see milestone photos, and review details — without emailing you.

The commission pipeline replaced the spreadsheet. The client portal replaced the status-update emails. And the Shopify sync meant no more double-entry between systems.

If you're a jewelry studio owner on Shopify, I'd genuinely love your feedback — what's missing? What would make this more useful for your workflow? You can see more at jmsdevlab.com/jewelry.

How-To Post 2 — "Forecasting Cashflow in Shopify Without a Spreadsheet"

Post in: Shopify Community > Shopify Discussion > Managing Your Business

Title: Forecasting Cashflow in Shopify Without a Spreadsheet

I want to talk about something most Shopify merchants don't do — but probably should. Cashflow forecasting.

Here's what I've seen: merchants know their revenue, they know their costs (roughly), but they don't project forward. They check the bank balance and hope it looks okay. When a big expense hits — inventory restock, annual subscription renewals, tax payments — it's a surprise. Not because they didn't know it was coming, but because nobody sat down and modelled it out.

Some merchants do forecast, but it's a spreadsheet nightmare. You export Shopify orders into a CSV, manually enter your recurring costs, try to build formulas that project forward, and then never update it because it takes 45 minutes every time. The spreadsheet goes stale within a week.

The underlying approach isn't complicated though. You take your historical order data (Shopify already has this), layer in your known recurring costs (rent, subscriptions, payroll, loan repayments), add any one-off expenses you can see coming, and project your bank balance forward 30, 60, 90 days. The maths is simple — it's the data gathering and updating that kills people.

That's exactly why we built SmartCash. It pulls your Shopify order data automatically, lets you input your recurring and one-off costs, and generates a rolling cashflow forecast with AI-assisted projections. It updates itself as new orders come in, so the forecast stays current without you touching a spreadsheet.

The goal was to make cashflow forecasting something a merchant actually does — not something they tried once in January and abandoned by February.

If you're doing this manually today (or not doing it at all), I'd love to hear how you think about cashflow. What would a useful forecasting tool look like for your business? More details at smartcashapp.net.

How-To Post 3 — "Managing Repair Tickets in Shopify"

Post in: Shopify Community > Shopify Discussion > Managing Your Business

Title: Managing Repair Tickets in Shopify — A Better Way Than Paper

If you run a jewelry or watch repair shop on Shopify, I'm curious — how are you tracking repair jobs right now?

Most repair shops I've talked to use one of three systems: a paper logbook, a spreadsheet, or a general-purpose project management tool that wasn't built for repairs. Each has problems. Paper gets lost, is unsearchable, and gives you no reporting. Spreadsheets work until you have 50+ active repairs and need to quickly find every job waiting on a specific part. Project management tools are too generic — they don't understand the specific stages a repair goes through.

A repair has a natural pipeline: received, diagnosed, quoted, customer approved, in progress, ready for collection, collected. Each stage has different requirements. At diagnosis, you need to document the issue with photos. At quoting, you need to list parts and labour. At approval, you need the customer's sign-off. At ready, you need to notify the customer. Paper and spreadsheets can't automate any of that.

Then there's the customer communication problem. "Is my watch ready yet?" is probably the most common phone call in any repair shop. Every one of those calls costs you time that could be spent on actual repairs.

We built RepairDesk to solve this. It's a Shopify app that gives you a proper repair ticket pipeline with all those stages, photo documentation at each step, parts and cost tracking, and automated customer notifications when a job changes status. Customers can check their repair's progress online instead of calling you.

If you're a jeweler or watchmaker managing repairs, I'd really like to hear about your workflow. What's the biggest pain point? What would save you the most time? You can see what we built at repairdeskapp.net.

Template Responses

Short, helpful replies for common threads. Always adapt to the specific question — these are starting points, not scripts.

1. Someone asking about jewelry CRM / commission tracking

Commission tracking for custom jewelry is tricky because it's not just order management — you need to track the whole journey from initial consultation through design iterations to final delivery. A standard CRM won't have fields for things like stone selections, metal preferences, or milestone photos.

What I'd suggest: whatever tool you use, make sure it can track each commission as a pipeline (not just a list), store client preferences for repeat customers, and ideally give your customers a way to check status themselves. That last one alone will cut your "how's it going?" emails in half.

Full disclosure — we built an app for exactly this called JewelryStudioManager (jmsdevlab.com/jewelry). Happy to answer questions about the workflow either way.

2. Someone asking about valuation certificates

The biggest issue with valuation certificates is getting them insurance-ready. Insurers are surprisingly specific about what fields they need — replacement value, detailed item description, measurements, hallmarks, gemstone grading — and if any are missing, the certificate gets rejected and your customer has to come back.

If you're generating certificates manually (Word or PDF templates), it works at small volume but breaks down fast. You end up spending more time on paperwork than on the actual valuation. Look for something that pre-populates the required fields and lets customers access their certificates through a portal so they're not calling you for copies.

We built an app called Jewel Value specifically for this (jewelvalue.app) — but regardless of what tool you use, make sure it covers the insurance field requirements for your region.

3. Someone asking about repair tracking

Repair tracking needs a pipeline view, not a list. Each repair goes through specific stages — received, diagnosed, quoted, approved, in progress, ready, collected — and you need different information at each stage. A spreadsheet can list repairs, but it can't trigger a notification when a job moves to "ready for collection" or attach diagnosis photos to a specific ticket.

The other big win is automated customer notifications. If your customers get an SMS or email when their repair status changes, you'll see a massive drop in "is it ready yet?" calls. That alone is worth the switch from paper.

We built a Shopify app called RepairDesk for exactly this workflow (repairdeskapp.net). But even if you go with another solution, prioritise pipeline stages and automated notifications — those are the two things that save the most time.

4. Someone asking about cashflow management

Cashflow management in Shopify basically comes down to: what's coming in, what's going out, and what does that look like 30-60-90 days from now. Shopify's built-in reports show you revenue and payouts, but they don't factor in your costs — rent, subscriptions, payroll, inventory restocks, tax payments — so you're only seeing half the picture.

The simplest approach is to list all your recurring costs, add any upcoming one-off expenses, and subtract those from your projected revenue based on recent trends. If you can automate the revenue side by pulling from Shopify order data, the forecast stays current without manual updates.

We built SmartCash (smartcashapp.net) to do exactly this with AI-assisted projections. But even a basic spreadsheet forecast is better than checking your bank balance and hoping for the best.

5. Someone asking about staff training

Staff training in retail is tough because it's usually unstructured — shadow someone for a week, here's a binder, figure it out. The problem is you can't track what anyone's actually learned, new hires take weeks to get up to speed, and when someone leaves, you start from scratch.

What works better is breaking training into specific modules (opening procedures, POS system, product knowledge, customer service scripts, etc.) and tracking completion per team member. That way you know exactly where each person's gaps are, and you can onboard new staff consistently instead of relying on whoever's available to train them.

We built a Shopify app called StaffHub for this (jmsdevlab.com) — it handles training modules, progress tracking, and team management. Worth a look if you're scaling your team.

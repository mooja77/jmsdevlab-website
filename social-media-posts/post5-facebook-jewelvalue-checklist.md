## Post

What makes a valuation certificate insurance-ready? Here's the checklist most appraisers miss.

I spent 22 years in the jewellery retail business and dealt with hundreds of insurance valuations. The number of certificates that get rejected or queried by insurers is staggering — and it's almost always the same things missing.

Here's what every insurance-ready valuation certificate needs:

**The Basics (most people get these right):**
- Full item description (ring, necklace, bracelet, etc.)
- Metal type and purity (e.g., 18ct yellow gold, 950 platinum)
- Total weight of the piece
- Date of valuation

**The Details (where most certificates fail):**
- Individual stone specifications: carat weight, cut, colour, clarity for EACH stone
- Measurements: dimensions of the piece AND individual stones
- Photographs: minimum 3 angles, on a plain background, with scale reference
- Hallmark details: assay office mark, date letter, maker's mark
- Serial numbers or unique identifying features

**The Professional Bits (where insurers push back):**
- Replacement value methodology: "like for like" replacement at current retail, not second-hand
- Appraiser credentials: qualifications, professional body membership
- Statement of purpose: "This valuation is prepared for insurance purposes"
- Validity period: most insurers require revaluation every 2-3 years
- Declaration: signed statement that the appraiser has physically examined the piece

**Common rejection reasons I've seen:**
- "Replacement value" stated without explaining the methodology
- No photographs (some insurers now require them)
- Stone grades listed without specifying the grading system used (GIA vs IGI vs in-house)
- Valuation older than 3 years at time of claim

If you're doing valuations regularly, having a system that ensures you never miss any of these fields saves an enormous amount of back-and-forth with insurers.

I actually built a tool for this — JewelValue (jewelvalue.com) — that walks you through every required field and generates the certificate automatically. But even without a tool, just printing this checklist and keeping it beside your bench will catch most of the gaps.
